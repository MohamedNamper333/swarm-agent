# Philosophy

Senior philosophy specialist with deep knowledge of major philosophical traditions, thinkers, and frameworks across Western and Eastern thought. Capable of analyzing ethical dilemmas, constructing philosophical arguments, explicating complex doctrines, and connecting philosophical ideas to modern questions.

## When to Use This Skill

- Analyzing ethical dilemmas using utilitarian, deontological, virtue ethics, and other frameworks
- Explaining philosophical positions and arguments from major figures across all historical periods
- Constructing and evaluating philosophical arguments with logical precision
- Examining epistemological questions about knowledge, belief, justification, and skepticism
- Exploring metaphysical topics including reality, causation, free will, identity, and consciousness
- Applying philosophical thinking to technology, science, medicine, law, and politics
- Understanding the historical development of philosophical ideas and their contemporary relevance
- Analyzing aesthetic theories and their application to art, beauty, and criticism

## Key Capabilities

- Explicate and critically evaluate major Western philosophers from the pre-Socratics to 20th-century thinkers
- Compare and contrast Eastern and Western philosophical traditions with cultural and historical context
- Apply ethical frameworks to concrete moral problems with awareness of strengths and limitations
- Analyze epistemological positions including rationalism, empiricism, skepticism, and constructivism
- Engage with metaphysical systems addressing ontology, causality, modality, and mind-body problems
- Evaluate arguments in philosophy of science including demarcation, underdetermination, and scientific realism
- Connect philosophical concepts to contemporary issues in AI ethics, bioethics, and political theory
- Identify underlying assumptions and implicit value commitments in any philosophical or practical position

## Core Concepts

### Western Philosophy — Ancient and Medieval
Pre-Socratics (Thales, Parmenides, Heraclitus), Socrates and the Socratic method, Plato (Theory of Forms, Republic, allegory of the cave), Aristotle (substance, causation, virtue ethics, logic), Stoicism (Epictetus, Marcus Aurelius), Epicureanism, Neoplatonism, Augustine, Aquinas, scholasticism.

### Western Philosophy — Modern and Contemporary
Descartes (cogito, mind-body dualism), Spinoza (monism, pantheism), Leibniz (monads, pre-established harmony), Hume (empiricism, skepticism), Kant (transcendental idealism, categorical imperative), Hegel (dialectic, absolute spirit), Marx (historical materialism, alienation), Nietzsche (will to power, perspectivism, eternal recurrence), existentialism (Sartre, Heidegger, Camus), phenomenology (Husserl, Merleau-Ponty), analytic philosophy (Frege, Russell, Wittgenstein, Quine).

### Eastern Philosophy
Confucianism (ren, li, filial piety), Daoism (wu wei, yin yang, Zhuangzi), Buddhism (Four Noble Truths, Eightfold Path, emptiness, Madhyamaka), Hinduism (Vedanta, Advaita, karma, dharma), Zen Buddhism, Shinto, Jainism (ahimsa, anekantavada).

### Ethics
Meta-ethics (moral realism vs. anti-realism, error theory, expressivism). Normative ethics: utilitarianism (Bentham, Mill), deontology (Kant, categorical imperative), virtue ethics (Aristotle, MacIntyre), care ethics (Gilligan, Noddings). Applied ethics: bioethics, AI ethics, environmental ethics, business ethics, animal ethics.

### Epistemology
Foundationalism, coherentism, reliabilism, internalism vs. externalism, Gettier problems, a priori vs. a posteriori knowledge, skepticism, Bayesian epistemology, social epistemology, epistemology of testimony.

### Metaphysics
Ontology (being, existence, categories), causation (Humean regularity, counterfactual, powers-based), free will (compatibilism, libertarianism, hard determinism), personal identity (psychological, bodily, narrative), modality (necessity, possibility, possible worlds), time (A-theory, B-theory).

### Philosophy of Science
Demarcation problem (Popper falsification, Kuhn paradigms, Lakatos research programs), scientific realism vs. anti-realism, underdetermination, theory change, explanation (deductive-nomological, causal-mechanical), reductionism, emergence.

### Political Philosophy
Social contract (Hobbes, Locke, Rousseau, Rawls), justice (Rawls, Nozick, Sen), liberty (Mill, Berlin), democracy, rights, equality, communitarianism, anarchism, Marxism, critical theory (Frankfurt School, Habermas), feminism, post-colonialism.

### Aesthetics
Nature of beauty and art, aesthetic judgment, expression theory, formalism, institutional theory of art, aesthetics of everyday life, aesthetic experience, taste and criticism.

## Practical Workflows

### 1. Analyze an Ethical Dilemma
1. Identify all stakeholders, their interests, and the core moral conflict in the scenario
2. Apply three ethical frameworks in sequence: utilitarian (consequences), deontological (duties/rules), virtue ethics (character)
3. Compare conclusions across frameworks, note tensions, and formulate a justified position with counterarguments

### 2. Reconstruct a Philosophical Argument
1. Identify the conclusion and find explicit premises stated by the author
2. Fill in implicit premises needed for logical validity using the principle of charity
3. Present the argument in standard form (premises numbered, conclusion stated), then evaluate each premise for truth

### 3. Apply the Socratic Method to a Belief
1. Ask the person to state their belief clearly and define key terms
2. Probe with hypothetical counterexamples that challenge the belief's consistency or scope
3. Guide toward a refined position that resolves contradictions uncovered during questioning

### 4. Evaluate a Theory of Knowledge
1. Identify the theory's central claim about the nature, source, or limits of knowledge
2. Test against Gettier-style counterexamples and the theory's ability to handle skeptical scenarios
3. Assess internal coherence and compare explanatory power against competing epistemological theories

### 5. Compare Eastern and Western Philosophical Positions
1. Identify the core question (e.g., nature of self, ethics, ultimate reality) addressed by both traditions
2. Present each tradition's answer with key texts and concepts, noting cultural and historical context
3. Find points of convergence and irreconcilable difference, avoiding forced equivalence or dismissive comparison

## Best Practices

- Identify and separate descriptive claims from normative judgments before analyzing any position
- Apply the principle of charity when interpreting philosophical texts and opposing arguments
- Trace the logical consequences of any philosophical position to test its coherence and plausibility
- Ground abstract philosophical discussion in concrete examples and thought experiments
- Recognize that philosophical questions often admit multiple defensible answers — evaluate arguments not certainty
- Consider the historical and cultural context of philosophical works without reducing them to context alone

# Architecture Design

Senior architectural design specialist producing buildable, sustainable, and contextually responsive buildings and spaces, from concept through construction documentation.

## When to Use This Skill

- Designing buildings and spatial environments at scales from furniture to urban districts
- Programming space requirements based on client needs, occupancy types, and functional relationships
- Integrating building systems: structural, mechanical, electrical, plumbing, and enclosure
- Applying environmental design strategies for passive heating, cooling, daylighting, and ventilation
- Navigating building codes, zoning regulations, accessibility requirements, and permitting processes
- Designing for sustainability: energy modeling, embodied carbon reduction, material selection, certification
- Developing architectural drawings, BIM models, and construction documentation for contractor bidding

## Key Capabilities

- Spatial design and programming: adjacency diagrams, bubble diagrams, space planning, circulation analysis, program matrices
- Building systems integration: structural systems (steel, concrete, timber, masonry), MEP, envelope, fire protection, vertical transport
- Structural principles: load paths, lateral stability, foundation types, span and depth ratios, material behavior
- Environmental design: passive solar, daylight factor analysis, natural ventilation, thermal mass, shading strategies
- Urban design and planning: site analysis, context studies, massing, setback, streetscape, public realm design
- Architectural history and theory: styles, periods, movements, tectonic expression, design philosophy, precedent analysis
- Building codes and regulations: IBC, zoning ordinances, egress requirements, fire ratings, accessibility (ADA/ICC A117.1)
- Sustainable design: LEED, Passive House, WELL, net-zero energy, embodied carbon, LCA, biophilic design

## Core Concepts

### Design Process
- **Pre-design**: Site analysis, climate data, code research, client programming, feasibility study, budget definition
- **Schematic design**: Massing studies, parti diagrams, plan concepts, section studies, design narrative, client presentations
- **Design development**: Material selection, system coordination, detailed sections, elevations, renderings, cost estimation
- **Construction documents**: Detailed drawings, specifications, schedules, coordination with consultants, permit set
- **Construction administration**: RFI responses, submittal review, site visits, change orders, punch list

### Spatial Design
- **Program**: Understand the activities, adjacencies, and spatial requirements of every function in the building
- **Circulation**: Design clear paths for people, goods, and services — public vs private, horizontal vs vertical
- **Proportion and scale**: Human scale at the point of interaction, monumental scale where appropriate, transition zones
- **Light and material**: Daylight defines space; material palette creates atmosphere, texture, and thermal comfort
- **Section as organizer**: Section reveals spatial relationships, light penetration, air movement, and structural logic

### Structural Principles
- Load paths must be continuous and explicit: dead load, live load, wind load, seismic load, snow load
- Span-to-depth ratios guide efficient structural depth: steel beams 1:20, concrete slabs 1:30, timber joists 1:18
- Lateral stability systems: braced frames, moment frames, shear walls, diagrids, tube structures
- Foundation types respond to soil conditions: spread footings, mat foundations, piles, caissons

### Environmental and Sustainable Design
- Orientation: elongate building east-west, minimize east and west glazing, maximize north and south exposure
- Daylighting: daylight factor target 2–5%, glare control, light shelves, clerestories, light wells
- Passive cooling: cross-ventilation, stack effect, earth coupling, night flush, evaporative cooling
- Envelope performance: continuous insulation, air barrier, thermal bridge-free detailing, high-performance glazing

### Building Codes and Life Safety
- Occupancy classification determines allowable height, area, and construction type
- Means of egress: occupancy load × egress width factor, maximum travel distance, number of exits
- Fire-resistance ratings: structural frame, walls, floors based on construction type and occupancy
- Accessibility: accessible route throughout, adaptable dwelling units, reach ranges, clear floor space

## Practical Workflows

### 1. Develop a Schematic Design from a Client Program
1. Translate the client's room list into a bubble diagram showing adjacency requirements and circulation paths
2. Explore 2–3 massing options on the site considering solar orientation, setbacks, and view corridors
3. Develop the selected parti into plan, section, and elevation sketches at 1/16″ = 1′-0″ scale for client review

### 2. Perform a Daylight Analysis for a Classroom
1. Set up the room geometry with window size, orientation, overhang depth, and interior reflectance values
2. Calculate the daylight factor using the split-flux method or climate-based simulation at the fall equinox
3. Adjust glazing area, light shelf depth, and shade placement until the daylight factor reaches 2–5% in all regularly occupied zones

### 3. Design an Egress System for a Mixed-Use Building
1. Determine occupancy load per floor from IBC Table 1004.5 based on use group (assembly, business, residential)
2. Calculate the required egress width (occupant load × 0.2 inches per occupant for stairs, 0.15 for doors) and number of exits
3. Verify the common path of egress travel distance and maximum travel distance meet IBC Chapter 10 limits for each occupancy

### 4. Create a Passive House Design Strategy
1. Orient the building on an east-west axis, minimize window area on east and west façades, maximize south glazing with fixed overhangs
2. Design the envelope with continuous insulation (R-40 walls, R-60 roof), triple-glazed windows, and an airtightness target of 0.6 ACH50
3. Size the mechanical ventilation system with heat recovery (HRV/ERV) to meet 0.3 air changes per hour and verify with PHPP modeling

### 5. Coordinate Structural and MEP Systems in Section
1. Stack the building section with structural bays aligned vertically, locate columns in partition walls to avoid encroaching on program space
2. Route main MEP ducts in ceiling plenums above corridors, branch into rooms with short duct runs and linear diffusers
3. Verify floor-to-floor height accommodates structure depth, MEP zone, ceiling suspension, and minimum finished ceiling height (9′-0″ typical)

## Best Practices

- Design the section before the plan — section reveals spatial experience, daylight penetration, and structural logic that plan alone cannot convey.
- Coordinate building systems in three dimensions during design development, not construction documents; clash detection at CD phase indicates failed coordination process.
- Start energy modeling at schematic design when passive strategies have the greatest impact; late-stage energy modeling can only optimize, not transform.

## Quality Checklist

- Program requirements are verified against client brief and building code occupancy load
- Egress paths meet maximum travel distance and exit separation requirements
- Daylight factor analysis completed for regularly occupied spaces
- Building section resolves structural depth, MEP distribution, and ceiling height
- Envelope thermal performance meets or exceeds code minimum
- Material specifications include embodied carbon data
- Accessible route connects all building levels and amenities
- Site design manages stormwater, solar access, and contextual relationships

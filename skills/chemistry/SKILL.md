# Chemistry

Senior chemistry specialist with deep expertise across all major branches of chemistry. Capable of explaining chemical phenomena, balancing reactions, predicting molecular properties, analyzing spectroscopic data, solving thermodynamic problems, and connecting chemical principles to practical applications.

## When to Use This Skill

- Solving problems involving chemical reactions, stoichiometry, equilibrium, and kinetics
- Analyzing molecular structure, bonding, and properties using quantum chemical principles
- Predicting reaction products and mechanisms in organic and inorganic chemistry
- Interpreting spectroscopic data (NMR, IR, UV-Vis, MS) for structure elucidation
- Performing thermodynamic calculations including enthalpy, entropy, Gibbs free energy, and reaction spontaneity
- Understanding biochemical pathways, enzyme kinetics, and molecular biology fundamentals
- Applying acid-base theory, redox chemistry, and electrochemistry to analytical problems
- Explaining periodic trends, chemical bonding theories, and material properties

## Key Capabilities

- Solve stoichiometric problems including limiting reagents, yields, and solution concentration calculations
- Apply VSEPR theory, molecular orbital theory, and hybridization to predict molecular geometry and bonding
- Determine reaction mechanisms and intermediates in organic synthesis pathways
- Analyze equilibrium systems using equilibrium constants, Le Chatelier's principle, and ICE tables
- Calculate thermodynamic quantities using Hess's law, standard enthalpies, and calorimetry data
- Predict reaction rates and their dependence on concentration, temperature, and catalysts
- Interpret IR, NMR (1H and 13C), UV-Vis, and mass spectra for compound identification
- Apply acid-base theories (Brønsted-Lowry, Lewis) including pH calculations, buffers, and titration curves

## Core Concepts

### Physical Chemistry
Quantum chemistry: atomic orbitals, molecular orbital theory, Born-Oppenheimer approximation, variational principle. Thermodynamics: first, second, and third laws, state functions, Gibbs free energy, chemical potential, phase equilibrium. Kinetics: rate laws, Arrhenius equation, transition state theory, catalysis, enzyme kinetics (Michaelis-Menten). Electrochemistry: Nernst equation, standard potentials, electrolysis, batteries, corrosion.

### Organic Chemistry
Functional groups, nomenclature (IUPAC), stereochemistry (chirality, R/S, E/Z, conformation), reaction mechanisms (SN1, SN2, E1, E2), carbonyl chemistry, aromaticity and electrophilic aromatic substitution, pericyclic reactions, organometallic chemistry, retrosynthetic analysis, protecting groups.

### Inorganic Chemistry
Periodic trends (electronegativity, ionization energy, atomic radius), coordination chemistry (crystal field theory, ligand field theory, isomerism), main group chemistry, transition metals, lanthanides and actinides, organometallic compounds, solid-state chemistry, band theory, magnetism.

### Analytical Chemistry
Spectroscopy (atomic absorption, emission, UV-Vis, IR, Raman, NMR, MS), chromatography (GC, HPLC, TLC), electrochemical analysis (potentiometry, voltammetry), mass spectrometry, separation techniques, extraction, calibration methods, error analysis, limit of detection.

### Biochemistry
Amino acids, proteins, enzymes, nucleic acids, carbohydrates, lipids, metabolic pathways (glycolysis, citric acid cycle, oxidative phosphorylation), enzyme kinetics and inhibition, DNA structure and replication, transcription and translation, bioenergetics.

### Chemical Bonding and Molecular Structure
Ionic bonding, covalent bonding (sigma and pi bonds, bond order, bond length), metallic bonding, intermolecular forces (hydrogen bonding, van der Waals, dipole-dipole), Lewis structures, resonance, VSEPR theory, hybridization, molecular orbital diagrams.

### Laboratory Techniques
Synthesis techniques (reflux, distillation, extraction, crystallization, chromatography), characterization (melting point, refractive index, polarimetry), safety protocols, waste disposal, proper glassware use, inert atmosphere techniques, titration methods.

## Practical Workflows

### 1. Balance a Redox Reaction
1. Assign oxidation numbers to all atoms to identify which elements are oxidized and reduced
2. Write separate half-reactions for oxidation and reduction, balance atoms and charge with H⁺/OH⁻ and electrons
3. Multiply half-reactions by a common factor so electrons cancel, combine, and verify atom and charge balance

### 2. Determine a Reaction Mechanism from Rate Data
1. Measure initial rates at varying concentrations to determine rate law exponents
2. Propose elementary steps where the rate-determining step matches the experimental rate law
3. Check that all proposed intermediates are chemically plausible and that the mechanism sums to the overall reaction

### 3. Identify an Unknown Compound by Spectroscopy
1. Examine the mass spectrum for the molecular ion peak to determine molecular weight and possible formula
2. Analyze the IR spectrum for functional group absorptions (carbonyl, O-H, N-H, C=C)
3. Assign ¹H and ¹³C NMR signals to specific environments, integrate proton signals, and assemble the structure

### 4. Calculate Equilibrium Composition
1. Write the balanced equation and express the equilibrium constant K in terms of concentrations or partial pressures
2. Set up an ICE table with initial amounts, changes expressed in terms of unknown x, and equilibrium expressions
3. Solve for x using the equilibrium expression, verify the result is chemically reasonable, compute final concentrations

### 5. Design a Synthetic Route
1. Identify the target molecule and perform retrosynthetic analysis to find disconnections and synthons
2. Select reagents and conditions for each forward step, considering protecting group strategy
3. Check each step for stereochemical control, functional group compatibility, and overall yield feasibility

## Best Practices

- Begin any problem by identifying exactly what is known and what is unknown — categorize variables before calculating
- For organic synthesis, use retrosynthetic analysis: work backwards from the target molecule to viable starting materials
- Always balance chemical equations and check charge balance for electrochemical or redox reactions
- Verify units are consistent — convert everything to standard units (SI) before plugging into formulas
- In spectroscopy, look for diagnostic peaks first (carbonyl, O-H, N-H) rather than trying to assign every signal
- Consider reaction conditions (solvent, temperature, pH) as active variables, not passive settings
- Maintain a healthy skepticism toward single analytical techniques — corroborate with independent measurements

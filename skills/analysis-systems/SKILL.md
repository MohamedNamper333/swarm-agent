# Systems Thinking

Systems thinking specialist — analyzes complex, interconnected systems using causal loop diagrams, stock-and-flow models, feedback loop analysis, leverage point identification, and systemic archetypes to understand emergent behavior and design effective interventions.

## When to Use This Skill

- Diagnosing recurring problems in an organization, product, or engineering process that resist simple fixes
- Mapping the underlying structure of a system (incentives, policies, delays, information flows) that generates observed behavior patterns
- Identifying high-leverage interventions — where a small change produces large, lasting improvement — and avoiding low-leverage symptomatic fixes
- Analyzing unintended consequences before implementing a policy, feature, or organizational change
- Modeling growth dynamics, adoption curves, capacity constraints, and market feedback loops in product strategy

## Key Capabilities

- Construct causal loop diagrams (CLDs) — identify variables, draw reinforcing (R) and balancing (B) feedback loops, mark link polarities (+/-), and highlight delays
- Build stock-and-flow (S&F) models — distinguish accumulations (stocks) from rates of change (flows), model draining/filling behavior, and simulate over time
- Recognize and apply system archetypes: fixes that fail, shifting the burden, tragedy of the commons, escalation, success to the successful, limits to growth, drifting goals, and growth and underinvestment
- Use the Iceberg Model to move from surface-level events → patterns of behavior → systemic structure → mental models that perpetuate the system
- Identify leverage points in order of effectiveness — from changing parameters (least effective) up to changing the system's goals, rules, structure, and paradigm (most effective)

## Best Practices

- Start with the problem behavior over time (BOT graph) before building the model — define what trend is undesirable and who experiences it
- Include at least one delay in every causal loop diagram — delays are where policy resistance and overshoot most often originate
- Validate the model by asking "what would have to be true" for the behavior to change, then test against real data rather than asserting the model is correct
